<details> <summary><strong>April 2025 — RLVR: Does Reinforcement Learning Really Incentivize Reasoning?</strong></summary>

Paper: arXiv

Authors: Yang Yue et al.

Summary: The paper evaluates RL with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR) and finds that it does not extend reasoning capabilities beyond the base model. Instead, RLVR primarily improves sampling efficiency — making the model more likely to output correct answers it already “knows.”
In contrast, distillation techniques actually improve reasoning capacity.

</details> <details> <summary><strong>June 2025 — The Illusion of Thinking: Understanding the Strengths and Limitations of Reasoning Models via the Lens of Problem Complexity</strong></summary>

Paper: TODO

Summary: Examines how LRMs (Large Reasoning Models) allocate computational resources relative to problem complexity. Findings show that they overthink simple problems, underthink complex problems, and perform best on medium-difficulty tasks compared to LLMs.
Additionally, LRMs struggle with algorithmic tasks like the Tower of Hanoi, failing to follow step-by-step algorithmic instructions properly.

</details> <details> <summary><strong>June 2025 — Potemkin Understanding in LLMs</strong></summary>

Paper: arXiv

Institutions: MIT, Harvard & University of Chicago

Summary: Introduces the concept of “Potemkin Understanding”, where LLMs produce correct answers without genuine conceptual understanding — like a facade that looks convincing but is structurally hollow.
Critiques common evaluation methods that equate correct answers with human-like understanding, highlighting that models may simply mimic outputs rather than grasp concepts.
