## 🧠 Reasoning

### April 2025 — RLVR: Does Reinforcement Learning Really Incentivize Reasoning?
**Paper:** [arXiv:2504.13837](https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.13837)  
**Summary:**  
The paper evaluates RL with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR) and finds that it does **not** extend reasoning capabilities beyond the base model.  
Instead, RLVR primarily improves sampling efficiency — making the model more likely to output correct answers it already “knows.”  
In contrast, distillation techniques actually improve reasoning capacity.  
A critical post on LinkedIn challenges and expands on this paper’s conclusions.

### 2025 — DeepSeek-R1: Reinforcement Learning for Reasoning
**Paper:** [arXiv:2501.12948](https://arxiv.org/abs/2501.12948)  
**Summary:** Introduces *Grouped Related Policy Optimization (GRPO)*, a novel RL method that enhances the reasoning capabilities of pretrained models.  
DeepSeek-R1 combines supervised fine-tuning (SFT) and RL, and finds that for small models, distillation from larger models outperforms RL for improving reasoning.

### June 2025 — The Illusion of Thinking: Understanding the Strengths and Limitations of Reasoning Models via the Lens of Problem Complexity
**Paper:** [arXiv](https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.06941) 
**Summary:**  
Examines how large reasoning models allocate computational resources relative to problem complexity.  
Finds that they overthink simple problems, underthink hard ones, and perform best on medium-difficulty tasks.  
Also highlights failures on algorithmic tasks like Tower of Hanoi, where step-by-step reasoning is mishandled.

### June 2025 — Potemkin Understanding in LLMs
**Paper:** [arXiv:2506.21521](https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.21521)  
**Institutions:** MIT, Harvard & University of Chicago  
**Summary:** Introduces the concept of “Potemkin Understanding”, where LLMs produce correct answers without genuine conceptual understanding — like a facade that looks convincing but is structurally hollow.
Critiques common evaluation methods that equate correct answers with human-like understanding, highlighting that models may simply mimic outputs rather than grasp concepts.
